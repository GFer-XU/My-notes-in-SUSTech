# A Review of BVOC Emission Factors from Vegetation in Southern China Over the Past 20 Years: Literature Synthesis and Proposed Outline

## TL;DR

- **The literature on BVOC emission factors (EFs) in southern China (Guangdong, Guangxi, Hainan, Hong Kong, Macao) over 2004–2025 has matured from a sparse, seedling- and static-enclosure-dominated phase (Klinger et al., 2002; Tsui et al., 2009; Leung et al., 2010; Zheng et al., 2010) to a dynamic-chamber, mature-tree, multi-compound phase led by the Guangzhou Institute of Geochemistry (Zeng/Zhang/Wang group), Jinan University (Wang Xuemei/Chang Ming), Hainan rainforest mobile-monitoring teams, and the IAP/CAS (Bai Jianhui) REA flux community — with the consistent and consequential finding that locally measured EFs reduce modelled total BVOC emissions in south China relative to MEGAN defaults (notably ~40% lower for sesquiterpenes), but raise EFs for some monoterpenes by 1–27× over MEGAN defaults in tropical Hainan.**
- **A regional review is now timely and distinct from existing China- or Asia-wide reviews (Li et al., 2020; Mu et al., 2022; Pei et al., 2025; Luo et al., 2025) because (i) southern China contributes the highest per-area BVOC emissions in the country (driven by Eucalyptus, Ficus, Syzygium, Hevea, bamboo and rainforest broadleaves), (ii) it exhibits unique stress drivers (typhoons, heatwaves, urban heat islands of the GBA, severe N deposition), and (iii) recent 2022–2026 papers introduce age-dependent EFs, heatwave-suppressed isoprene optima, drone-LiDAR canopy mapping, and the GEMBE empirical model — none of which has been synthesised in a region-focused review.**
- **The single most important, decision-relevant conclusion from the literature is that emission-factor uncertainty (a factor of 3–5 across studies in China; up to one order of magnitude in measured-vs-modelled isoprene mixing ratios) — not algorithm structure or land-cover data alone — remains the dominant source of error in BVOC inventories for the Pearl River Delta/Greater Bay Area, and therefore continued localized EF measurement on dominant species (especially sesquiterpenes, OVOCs, mature trees, and stress responses) is the highest-leverage research investment for improving regional ozone and SOA modelling.**

## Key Findings

### 1. Three Generations of EF Measurement in Southern China

**Generation 1 (2001–2010): foundational, mostly static enclosure / seedling.**
- *Klinger, Li, Guenther, Greenberg, Baker, Bai (2002, JGR-Atmospheres, 107(D21), 4603)*: PID screening + leaf cuvette/GC-MS at 52 sites including subtropical Dinghu Mountain (Guangdong), and tropical Xishuangbanna; 386 of 541 species sampled; identified Hevea brasiliensis as a strong light-dependent monoterpene emitter; reported total Chinese biogenic VOC emissions comparable to Europe; uncertainty ~factor of 3.
- *Yang et al., 2001 (China Environ. Sci. 21, 422–426)*: first hydrocarbon-from-natural-source study in the Pearl River Delta.
- *Zhao, Bai, Wang & Zhang (2004, China Environ. Sci. 24(6), 654–657)*: early "Studies on the emission rates of plants VOCs in China" — among the earliest Chinese-language VOC EF measurements.
- *Wang Y.-F., Owen, Li & Peñuelas (2007, Global Change Biology, 13(11), 2270–2282)*: Hevea brasiliensis at Xishuangbanna; sabinene, α-pinene, β-pinene dominant; cis-ocimene shows circadian (not light/T) control. Foundational for rubber tree EFs used in Hainan inventories.
- *Wilske et al. (2007, Atmos. Environ.)*: Eight tropical tree species at Xishuangbanna using dynamic Teflon bag enclosures; Garcinia cowa and Celtis philippensis emit isoprene at ~20 µg g⁻¹ h⁻¹.
- *Tsui, Guenther, Yip & Chen (2009, Atmos. Environ., from a 2004 EPD-HKU project)*: 13 Hong Kong tree species; first BVOC inventory for HKSAR — 8.6 × 10⁹ g C yr⁻¹ (isoprene 30%, monoterpenes 40%, OVOCs 30%); ten species accounted for 76% of total annual emissions.
- *Leung, Wong, Tsui, Kwok & Lim (2010, Atmos. Environ.)*: improved Hong Kong land cover and EFs; ten additional plant species measured under controlled conditions; MEGAN adapted to HKSAR.
- *Zheng, Zheng, Yu & Zhong (2010, Atmos. Environ., 44, 1960–1969)*: temporal and spatial characteristics and uncertainty of biogenic VOC emissions in the PRD — the foundational PRD biogenic VOC inventory paper, widely cited.

**Generation 2 (2011–2018): MEGAN constrained with local EFs and PFTs; canopy/box studies.**
- *Wang, Situ, Guenther, Chen, Fan, Chang et al. (2011, Tellus B 63(2), 241–254)*: 4-km gridded MEGAN run for the PRD using local plant functional types from Thematic Mapper and local-survey data; annual: isoprene 95.55 × 10⁶ kg C, monoterpenes 117.35 × 10⁶ kg C, sesquiterpenes 9.77 × 10⁶ kg C; using MEGAN default EFs led to a 24% increase, demonstrating localized EFs are critical.
- *Huang et al. (2011)* and *Wang et al. (2003, 2014)*: branch enclosure / leaf cuvette measurements of urban and suburban species in PRD/Hong Kong; mostly isoprene + total monoterpenes.
- *Situ, Guenther, Wang, Jiang, Turnipseed, Wu, Bai, Wang (2013, ACP, 13, 11803–11817; 2014 Atmos. Environ.)*: WRF-Chem/MEGAN PRD; demonstrated MEGAN over-predicts isoprene in autumn even with localized EFs and land cover; identified land cover and EFs as the most sensitive drivers; canopy flux measurements in PRD show simulations overestimate both isoprene and monoterpenes.
- *Wang, Situ, Chen, Zheng, Guenther, Fan, Chang (2016, J. Environ. Sci.)*: numerical-model PRD review summarising history of BVOC modelling and ranking emission factors as the largest contributor to model uncertainty.
- *Bai, Guenther, Turnipseed & Duhl (2015, 2016a, 2017)*: REA + gradient + enclosure measurements at Qianyanzhou subtropical Pinus plantation (Jiangxi, adjacent to southern China climate zone) and others; canopy-scale EFs of 0.71–1.19 mg m⁻² h⁻¹ (isoprene) and 1.39–1.65 mg m⁻² h⁻¹ (total monoterpenes); foundation for the EMBE empirical model.

**Generation 3 (2019–2025): mature trees, dynamic chambers, full speciation, stress-response, and high-resolution canopy mapping.**
- *Zeng, Zhang, Pang, Mu, Song, Guo, Wang, Peñuelas (2022, Front. For. Glob. Change 5, 1089676)*: dynamic chambers + TD-GC/MS on 20 mature trees (15 broadleaf, 5 needle-leaf) in Guangdong; isoprene + 14 MTs + 9 SQTs. **Key EFs (μg g⁻¹ h⁻¹ dry leaf): Eucalyptus urophylla 26.47±3.70 (isoprene); Ficus hispida 20.74±1.78; Syzygium hainanense 20.49±1.36; Casuarina equisetifolia 18.70±2.91; Mangifera indica 11.71±7.04; Cunninghamia lanceolata low isoprene; Magnolia denudata α-pinene 8.33±2.05.** Localized canopy-scale EF totals for MTs (667 µg m⁻² h⁻¹) and SQTs (108 µg m⁻² h⁻¹) ~33% and ~41% lower than MEGAN v2.1 BETT defaults.
- *Zeng et al. (2023, Atmos. Environ.; Sept 2019–Oct 2019 PRD campaign on 8 species E. urophylla, S. jambos, F. hispida, A. auriculaeformis, C. hystrix, S. superba, A. confusa, P. massoniana)*: temperature/light dependence — G93 isoprene algorithm captures both isoprene and MTs from tropical/subtropical trees; previously assumed temperature-only MT algorithm fails in this region — a major modelling implication.
- *Wang N., Liu, Xu et al. (2023, JGR-Atmospheres, 128, e2023JD039928)*: applied Zeng et al. (2022a) localized EFs to MEGAN-CMAQ over south China — **localized EFs reduce regional BVOC emissions, particularly sesquiterpenes by ~40% on average; improved O3 and SOA model predictions in all seasons; substantial site-by-site differences persist between PRD and non-PRD Guangdong**.
- *Chen et al. (2023, J. Environ. Sci.)*: Mt. Nanling subtropical pristine forest, four altitudes (600–1690 m); broadleaf monoterpenes dominate; MT and SQT EFs in inventories underestimated by 2–58×. High emitters Schima superba, Yushania basihirsuta, Altingia chinensis.
- *Hainan tropical rainforest (Sci. Total Environ., 2024)*: vehicle-mounted mobile online PTR-MS-style monitoring on 30 species in the Hainan tropical rainforest. **Monoterpenes (sabinene, γ-terpinene, β-ocimene) dominate broadleaf emissions; isoprene EFs slightly below MEGAN defaults but MT/SQT EFs 1–27× higher than MEGAN; Hainan mature-forest BVOC inventory 244.43 Gg C yr⁻¹ in 2023; isoprene 74%, MTs 16%; July peak 30.08 Gg C yr⁻¹, January minimum 8.84 Gg C yr⁻¹.**
- *Bai et al. (2025, Atmos. Pollut. Res.; Atmos. Environ., 2025): tropical rubber plantation Danzhou, Hainan*. Whole-ecosystem BVOC and O₃ fluxes 2018–2020; isoprene fluxes 0.01–3.71 mg m⁻² h⁻¹ (mean 0.71); MTs −0.08 to 12.38 mg m⁻² h⁻¹; supports development of GEMBE (Generalized Empirical Model of BVOC Emissions), benchmarked to MEGAN.
- *Zeng, Zhang, Pang, Wang et al. (2025, GRL 52, e2025GL114767)*: **Heatwaves suppress the temperature optimum of isoprene emission from subtropical Eucalyptus** — tropical isoprene is more T-sensitive than temperate plants under moderate conditions, but extreme heat shifts and lowers the optimum, implying MEGAN over-predicts during extreme thermal events.
- *Zeng et al. (2025, EGUsphere preprint, egusphere-2025-3226)*: **Age-dependent BVOC emissions in Eucalyptus urophylla** — leaf cuvette and branch chamber give equivalent isoprene EFs (within 5%), but tree age (2-month seedling vs 2-year sapling vs mature) shifts terpene composition, with field-grown trees showing >85% β-ocimene of MT flux and double the SQT emission of seedlings; ~one-third of global EF database entries based on seedlings may misrepresent mature-tree emissions.
- *Zeng, Zhang, Mu et al. (2023, Front. For. Glob. Change)*: soil isoprenoid uptake in a Eucalyptus urophylla plantation — soils are net sinks for ambient isoprene under sub-tropical Chinese plantations.
- *Duan, Chang, Wu, Situ, Zhu, Zhang, Huangfu, Wang, Chen, Yuan, Wang (2024, AMT 17, 4065)*: drone-based LiDAR + photogrammetry + image recognition to estimate BVOC emissions in subtropical Guangdong forest — Myrtaceae top isoprene family, Rubiaceae top monoterpene family.
- *Mu, Llusià, Zeng, Zhang, Asensio, Yang, Yi, Wang, Peñuelas (2022, Front. Plant Sci. 13, 833030)*: review of isoprenoid emissions from tropical plant species — 58.3% of 108 Chinese tropical species are isoprene emitters.

### 2. Quantitative ranges of emission factors (compiled)

**Isoprene (μg g⁻¹ dry leaf h⁻¹, leaf-level standard 30 °C, 1000 µmol m⁻² s⁻¹):**
- Eucalyptus urophylla: 1.76–70.00 (Zeng et al. 2022 mean 26.44±22.73; their measurement 26.47±3.70).
- Ficus hispida: 20.74±1.78; Ficus virens (subtropical urban): 31.5 (Lv et al. 2021).
- Syzygium hainanense: 20.49±1.36; S. jambos: a strong isoprene emitter.
- Casuarina equisetifolia: 18.70±2.91.
- Mangifera indica: 11.71±7.04.
- Acacia auriculaeformis, A. confusa, Bombax ceiba, Bambusa ventricosa, Cryptomeria fortunei: dominated by isoprene (>50% of BVOC).
- Schima superba: ~7–24 pmol m⁻² s⁻¹ leaf area depending on T (10–40 °C); high emitter in Nanling pristine forest.
- Hevea brasiliensis: light-dependent monoterpene emitter rather than strong isoprene emitter (Wang et al. 2007).
- Pinus massoniana, P. elliottii, Cunninghamia lanceolata: low isoprene, moderate monoterpene; sabinene/3-carene/trans-β-ocimene below detection (vs MEGAN defaults of 70/160/70 µg m⁻² h⁻¹ for NETT — implying MEGAN over-estimates these compounds for needle-leaf trees in southern China).
- Cinnamomum camphora: low isoprene but very high D-limonene (93.6 µg gdw⁻¹ h⁻¹) — major SOA potential.
- Mangroves (15 species, Indo-West Pacific & Atlantic East Pacific): very low isoprenoid emitters; mangrove ecosystems weakly contribute to atmosphere–biosphere BVOC exchange (Sci. Total Environ., 2023).
- Bamboo: Phyllostachys violascens (Lei) and P. heterocycla (Moso) canopy-scale EFs ~3.3–3.6 mg m⁻² h⁻¹ isoprene, ~0.008–0.16 mg m⁻² h⁻¹ α-pinene (Bai et al. 2016; consistent with MEGAN v2.1).

**Canopy-scale total MT and SQT EFs from Zeng et al. (2022):** MTs 667 µg m⁻² h⁻¹; SQTs 108 µg m⁻² h⁻¹ (33%/41% lower than MEGAN v2.1 BETT defaults).

**Hainan canopy-scale (2024 mobile measurement):** localized EFs for MTs/SQTs are 1–27× higher than MEGAN defaults; isoprene slightly lower.

**Hong Kong (Tsui 2009 + Leung 2010):** 13–23 species; chamber at 30 °C, 1000 µmol m⁻² s⁻¹; data feed GloBEIS and MEGAN runs.

### 3. Major inventory studies and totals

- **Tsui et al. (2009)** — Hong Kong: 8.6 × 10⁹ g C yr⁻¹.
- **Zheng et al. (2010, Atmos. Environ.)** — PRD: foundational gridded biogenic VOC inventory for the PRD; widely used spatial template.
- **Wang et al. (2011, Tellus B)** — PRD 4 km × 4 km, hourly: isoprene 95.55 + monoterpenes 117.35 + sesquiterpenes 9.77 (×10⁶ kg C yr⁻¹).
- **Situ et al. (2013, ACP)** — PRD WRF-Chem/MEGAN; isoprene/MT seasonal/regional sensitivity.
- **Cui, Xian, Han, Shu, Qian, Ouyang, Wang (2024, J. Environ. Manage. 351, 119754)** — Shenzhen 2019: 3.84 × 10⁹ g C yr⁻¹, ~5.2% of municipal VOCs; 88.9% from less built-up areas; OFP 7.8% of total VOC OFP.
- **Wang N., Liu et al. (2023, JGR-Atmospheres)** — south China (Guangdong incl. PRD/non-PRD) updated MEGAN run with Zeng 2022 EFs; ~40% lower SQT emissions than default; improved O3 and SOA prediction.
- **Wang N. et al. (2025, ACP 25, 8859–8870)** — PRD 2001–2020: BVOC emissions increased significantly, with climate-driven contributions ~80% of the total increase; ~6.2 ppb O3 contribution that has weakened anthropogenic VOC reduction benefits.
- **Hainan mobile inventory (2024, STOTEN)** — Hainan dominant mature forest 2023: 244.43 Gg C yr⁻¹; 74% isoprene, 16% MTs.
- **Cao et al. (2024, JGR-Atmospheres)** — China-wide using HRVD vegetation: 15.66 Tg yr⁻¹ for 2020; emphasises south China hotspots.
- **Li, Yang, Xie, Wu (2020, STOTEN, 733, 139301)** — China 2008–2018: 58.89 Tg in 2018 (isoprene 63.6%, MT 11.4%, SQT 2.2%, OVOCs 22.9%); Hainan and southeast forests are emission hotspots; 20.18% increase 2008–2018.
- **MDPI Atmosphere 2026, MEGAN v3.2 China 2023:** 41.70 Tg yr⁻¹ (isoprene 26.90 + MTs 4.84 + SQTs 0.55 + OVOCs 9.41 Tg); Guangxi–Guangdong–Yunnan south–north gradient hotspot; OFP 346.12 Tg yr⁻¹, SOAFP 2137.51 Gg yr⁻¹.
- **Xi, Wang, Yuan, Feng, Zhao, Zhang & Wang (2025, GMD 18, 8627)** — SieMAC speciated isoprene emission model for China.
- **Zhu, Tang, Gao et al. (2022, Environ. Sci. Technol.)** — China-wide 1-km U-BVOC (urban green space) inventory; substantial fraction of urban total BVOCs in megacities.

### 4. Environmental drivers and stress

- **Temperature/light:** G93 algorithm captures both isoprene and MT in tropical/subtropical south China (Zeng 2023); previous T-only MT parameterisation systematically biased.
- **Drought:** Huang et al. (2023) — drought reduces total BVOC, MT, SQT in Pinus massoniana saplings but slightly increases isoprene; trade-off mediated by chlorophylls/starch/NSCs.
- **N deposition:** Eucalyptus urophylla in southern China — N50 nearly doubles BVOC vs N0 and N100; isoprene + α-pinene increase, d-limonene decreases at high N (Yuan et al. 2023). MEGAN under-estimates Eucalyptus emissions under elevated N, the typical south-China condition.
- **Smoke/forest fire:** Cunninghamia lanceolata and Schima superba release stress BVOCs after smoke exposure.
- **Wounding (litchi, longan, banana, mango):** wound-induced LOX products (hexenal, hexanol esters, methanol, acetaldehyde) dominate first-burst stress emission; methanol ~40%, hexenal ~24%, acetaldehyde ~11% (Front. Plant Sci. 2021 tropical agricultural species).
- **Heat waves:** Zeng et al. 2025 — heatwaves suppress the isoprene T optimum in Eucalyptus; biophysical limitation overrides Arrhenius behaviour.
- **Typhoon/tropical cyclones:** Wang et al. (2022, Sci. Adv. 8, eabl6166) — Typhoon Nesat (2017) increased isoprene by 94% over south-China forests; statistically, summer typhoon-period isoprene emissions are 78% higher than climatology, contributing to cross-regional O₃ pollution from PRD to YRD/Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei.
- **Extreme weather coupling:** Xu et al. (2024, ACP 24, 1559) — September 2022 PRD heatwave + typhoon periphery: hot weather ↑ BVOCs ~10%; isoprene + biogenic HCHO drove 47% of in-situ O3 production.
- **Ozone-induced BVOCs:** Gai et al. (2023, STOTEN) — elevated O3 over China 2014–2020 increased BVOC emissions by 104.7 Gg yr⁻¹ (isoprene −18.6%, MT +27.5%, SQT +40.4%, OVOCs +50.8%).

### 5. Methodological state of the art

- **Static enclosure (1990s–2000s):** dominant in Tsui 2009, Leung 2010 etc.; questionable for mature trees and CO2 depletion artefacts (Niinemets et al. 2011).
- **Dynamic branch chamber (Zeng et al. 2022 method):** ~1.7 min residence time, ozone-scrubbed circulating air, paired inlet/outlet sampling, sorbent-tube TD-GC/MS — current gold standard for southern China.
- **Leaf cuvette + LI-6400XT modified (Yuan et al. 2023):** suitable for seedlings but biased vs mature trees (Zeng 2025 preprint).
- **Canopy flux: REA, gradient, eddy covariance:** Bai et al. 2015–2017 (Qianyanzhou subtropical Pinus); Sarkar et al. 2020 (portable REA in 9 ecosystems including subtropical bamboo at Taihuyuan, tropical rubber at Danzhou).
- **Vehicle-mounted online PTR-style mobile system:** new for Hainan rainforest 30-species campaign (STOTEN 2024).
- **Drone-borne LiDAR/photogrammetry/image recognition** for canopy species mapping (Duan et al. 2024 AMT) — emerging method to scale tree-level EFs to landscape.
- **Vocus PTR-MS / PTR-TOF-MS:** rising use at southeastern Chinese mountain background sites (e.g. ACP 2024 Shanghuang Mountain; ACP 2025 subtropical coastal South China OVOC study) — enables sesquiterpenes, terpene oxidation products with up to six O atoms. OVOCs comprise 72–77% of total VOC concentrations at a coastal South China site.

### 6. Atmospheric implications

- **Isoprene OH reactivity is rising while AVOCs decline.** Long-term observations in Hong Kong 2013–2019 (Zhang et al., 2023, Atmos. Environ. 313, 120051) show isoprene OH-reactivity increased at 0.13±0.06 s⁻¹ yr⁻¹ vs declining anthropogenic VOC reactivity (−0.02±0.03 s⁻¹ yr⁻¹), driving rising HCHO.
- **BVOCs offset anthropogenic VOC reductions:** Wang et al. 2025 ACP — climate-driven BVOC emissions account for ~80% of 2001–2020 BVOC increase in PRD and contribute 6.2 ppb O3, weakening anthropogenic-VOC controls.
- **Urban green space contribution:** Guangzhou UGS-BVOC 666 Gg ≈ 90 Mg km⁻² ≈ 33.45% of (AVOC+BVOC) in urban core, raising MDA8 O3 by up to 8.9 ppb (Liu et al., 2024 Sci. Total Environ./Atmos. Res. — Wei et al. 2024).
- **SOA formation:** Lin et al. (2019, ACP 19, 14403) — SOAI tracers 10.8–49.3 ng m⁻³ at 9 PRD sites; SOAM dominant component (47.2 ng m⁻³); β-caryophyllene SOA tracers detectable. Cao et al. (2014, EST) — pinene-derived nitrooxy-organosulfates 52–151 ng m⁻³ in PRD PM2.5.
- **Chemical regime:** PRD urban core is VOC-limited, making O3 highly sensitive to BVOC emissions; suburban/rural areas NOx-limited.

### 7. Knowledge gaps and controversies

- **Sesquiterpene EFs are extremely scarce** outside Zeng 2022 (Guangdong 9 SQT species) and the 2024 Hainan mobile study; most national inventories rely on default fractions of total MTs.
- **OVOC EFs (methanol, acetone, acetaldehyde, MBO) are virtually absent for southern Chinese species** despite OVOCs being 22.87% of national BVOC emissions (Li 2020) and 70%+ of total ambient VOCs at some south-China sites.
- **Mangroves under-sampled in China:** the only published global mangrove screening (Borges et al. 2023, Atmos. Environ.) reported very low isoprenoid EFs across 15 species, but no measurements have been conducted in Guangdong/Guangxi/Hainan mangrove reserves.
- **Crop emissions (litchi, longan, banana, sugarcane) are essentially uncharacterised** for ambient-flux EFs in southern China — only fruit/wound chemistry is known.
- **Mature-tree vs seedling discrepancy:** Zeng 2025 preprint shows ~one-third of global EF database may be biased due to seedling-only measurements.
- **Algorithm controversy:** monoterpene emissions in tropical/subtropical south China are temperature- AND light-dependent (G93), contradicting the temperature-only β-factor convention used in many earlier PRD inventories.
- **Land cover input uncertainty:** Cao et al. 2024 (JGR) and Huang et al. 2024 (Atmos. Environ. 337, 120765) — using ESA 10-m land cover yields 52.9% higher BVOC than MODIS 500-m for similar climates, with cascading O3 simulation impacts.
- **Mismatch between modelled and measured isoprene mixing ratios** across Chinese sites can exceed one order of magnitude (Zhang et al. 2020).
- **Underrepresented species:** Cinnamomum, Magnolia denudata (high MT/SQT), Phoebe bournei, Castanopsis hystrix, Altingia chinensis, Bombax, palms (Arecaceae IspS now characterised), Ficus altissima, urban Osmanthus, Ginkgo all need more measurements under field conditions.

### 8. Existing reviews to differentiate against

- **Mu et al. (2022, Front. Plant Sci. 13, 833030)** — "An Overview of the Isoprenoid Emissions from Tropical Plant Species" — pan-tropical, plant-physiology focused.
- **Li et al. (2020, STOTEN)** — China 2008–2018 inventory paper with embedded review of BVOC observational data but limited regional focus on south China.
- **Pei, Wang, Shen, Wu (2025, MDPI Atmosphere, 16(7), 885)** — "Research on the Emission of BVOCs from Terrestrial Vegetation" — broad terrestrial review, not regional.
- **Luo, Lun, Gao, Wang, Yang, Su, Habibullah-Al-Mamun, Xu, Li, Li (2025, Toxics, 13, 364)** — global plant BVOC review with future prospects.
- **Li L., Bai G., Han, Wu, Xie, Xie (2024)** — "Localized BVOC emission inventory in China: a comprehensive review" — national scope.
- **Wang Xuemei et al. (2016, J. Environ. Sci.)** — methodology-focused review of model approaches in PRD.

The **distinct angle** for the proposed paper is therefore: a region-bounded (Guangdong + Guangxi + Hainan + HK + Macao), 2004–2025, EF-centric synthesis explicitly linking measurement methodology evolution → species-level EF compilations → inventory implications → ozone/SOA policy.

## Recommendations (for the review authorship)

1. **Anchor the paper around the 2022 Zeng et al. mature-tree dataset and the 2024 Hainan mobile dataset** — these are the two most impactful southern-China-specific advances of the past five years and provide the empirical backbone for any EF table. Position older Tsui/Leung/Klinger/Zheng papers as the historical baseline.
2. **Build a single master EF compilation table** with columns: species, family, growth form, location (province/site), measurement method, isoprene EF, dominant MTs + EFs, dominant SQTs + EFs, OVOCs (where available), reference. This will be the highest-impact deliverable for the community and will distinguish your paper from non-regional reviews.
3. **Include an explicit comparison panel: localized EF vs MEGAN v2.1, MEGAN v3.2 default**, showing systematic biases (sesquiterpenes ~40% lower than default in PRD; MTs 1–27× higher than default in Hainan rainforest) — these are the most policy-relevant single facts in the literature.
4. **Devote a full section to stress-induced emissions**, since this is where southern China is uniquely informative globally (typhoons, coastal heatwaves, severe N deposition) and where 2022–2025 papers have made the biggest advances.
5. **Be explicit about controversies** — particularly the temperature-only-β vs G93-T+L parameterisation for monoterpenes in the tropics, and the seedling-vs-mature-tree EF gap — and recommend specific protocol harmonisation.
6. **Recommend monitoring infrastructure**: at least one long-term REA/EC tower in (a) Hainan rainforest (Jianfengling or Bawangling), (b) a Eucalyptus plantation in Guangxi, (c) a Pinus massoniana / Cunninghamia lanceolata mixed stand in Guangdong, and (d) a coastal mangrove (e.g. Dongzhai Harbor or Zhanjiang).
7. **Benchmarks that should change the recommendation**: if a coordinated south-China BVOC EF dataset (≥50 mature-tree species across all five jurisdictions, dynamic chamber, full speciation incl. SQTs and OVOCs) becomes available, the focus of regional CTM modelling can shift from EF-uncertainty reduction to algorithm refinement and stress-response parameterisation.

## Caveats

- **Temporal coverage of measurements is uneven**: nearly all pre-2010 measurements used static enclosures or seedlings, which are now considered unreliable for mature-tree EFs (Niinemets 2011; Zeng 2022). The "20-year" review must explicitly flag this, because comparing pre-2010 and post-2020 EFs is not apples-to-apples.
- **Published numbers carry uncertainties of factor 3–5 across studies and up to one order of magnitude relative to ambient observations** (Zhang et al. 2020). Any single value in tables should be reported with its method, age class, and reference.
- **Several Hainan, Hong Kong, and Macao species are still extrapolated from Xishuangbanna or Amazonian analogues**, since direct measurements are missing — the review should make this clear rather than presenting MEGAN default values as if they were measured.
- **Some 2025–2026 papers are preprints or early-view** (e.g., Zeng 2025 EGUsphere preprint on age-dependent EFs; the 2026 China-2023 inventory in MDPI Atmosphere) and have not yet completed peer review at time of writing — flag and re-check before final submission.
- **Recent results indicating isoprene-emission-optimum suppression under heatwaves (Zeng 2025 GRL) and strong typhoon enhancement (Wang 2022 Sci. Adv.)** rest on small numbers of events; their generality across south China should be presented as suggestive rather than established.
- **Mangrove and fruit-crop EFs in the region are essentially absent** — the review will have to be candid that this is a gap rather than presenting global-mean values as representative of southern Chinese ecosystems.
- **The single 2026 MEGAN v3.2 China inventory total (41.70 Tg)** is much higher than the 2020 HRVD-based estimate (15.66 Tg) and the 2018 Li et al. estimate (58.89 Tg). These differences are largely methodological (vegetation dataset and EF library) and should be discussed rather than reconciled into a single "best" value.
